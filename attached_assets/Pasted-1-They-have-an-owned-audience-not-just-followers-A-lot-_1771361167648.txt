1) They have an owned audience (not just followers)
A lot of the highest trip counts correlate with email list size + community infrastructure.
Examples: large email lists (1k–6k+) and/or membership models + chapters + FB groups.


Communities like Hiker Babes / All Women All Trails / Hobbit Hikers are built to gather people IRL, which makes trips a natural extension.


Signal to look for: email list, paid membership, FB group, Substack, podcast subscribers, “link in bio” funnels.
2) They sell identity + belonging, not just a destination
The strongest notes are identity-driven:
“women outdoors,” “safety/support,” “LGBTQ+ trips,” “Latinas community,” “body positive,” etc.


Signal to look for: “community” language, safety/support positioning, repeated themes, strong “you belong here” brand.
3) They’ve already monetized trust (courses, coaching, memberships, affiliate)
Many top performers already sell something successfully:
courses/readings/coaching, paid clubs, affiliate-heavy creators, workshops, etc.


Signal to look for: existing paid offers, checkout links, affiliates, “join my club,” “courses,” “workshops,” “retreats.”
4) They have operational stamina + repeatability
The real difference between 1–3 trips and 10–30+ trips is the ability to run the same play over and over:
clear niche → repeat destinations → predictable launch cadence.


Signal to look for: “doing retreats for years,” past group travel, or full-time creator or community leader


A simple “Top Host” scoring rubric 
Score each lead 0–3 on each (max 18). Prioritize 13+.
Owned audience (email list/substack/podcast/FB group)
Niche clarity (one audience, one promise)
Community depth (membership, chapters, active group, IRL meetups)
Monetization proof (courses/coaching/membership/affiliate engine)
Event/retreat experience (has run paid IRL experiences before)
Partner-fit (wants leverage + logistics partner; not “I want to run my own trips end-to-end”)



How we find more of them (repeatable sourcing plays)
A) Mine “community-first” ecosystems (best yield)
These places over-index on the exact traits above:
Facebook Groups (women-specific niche groups, hobby communities, “meetups” groups)
Substack (writers with strong subscriber bases — your list already has Substack)
Podcasts (hosts with loyal listeners → high conversion)
Membership platforms (Patreon-style communities, paid clubs)
Coaching / creator-educator niches (fitness, wellness, spirituality, leadership)


Search keywords: “community”, “membership”, “meetups”, “retreat”, “join us”, “newsletter”, “subscribers”, “patreon”.
B) Look for travel-adjacent leaders (not travel influencers)
Your list shows huge success with:
outdoor safety educators, fitness leaders, book communities, niche identities (LGBTQ+, Latinas, moms who adventure, etc.)


These folks don’t need to be “travel accounts.” They need authority + gathering power.

C) Build a referral loop from your best communities
Your top community hosts often have:
chapters, ambassadors, local leaders, adjacent creators.


Add a structured ask:
“Who are 3 other leaders like you (same audience, different region) who run meetups/events?”


This is usually a faster path to more “Monique/Bunny-style” hosts than cold IG outreach.

D) Target “launch-ready” signals on social
Even on IG/TikTok, prioritize:
link-in-bio funnels
“join my newsletter”
“DM ‘TRIP’”
repeated CTAs
creators selling products weekly (not just posting)


Those are the creators with a machine, not just reach.
Practical next step: define two ICPs and run two pipelines
From your list, I’d split sourcing into:
Community Operators (chapters, groups, memberships)
 Goal: high lifetime value, lots of repeat trips
Creator Broadcasters (podcast/substack/IG huge)
 Goal: big spikes, repeat potential to integrate into their creator strategy


Then build outreach + enablement tailored to each (operators want structure + safety + scalable templates; broadcasters want a clean promo calendar + strong assets + minimal friction).
